JAPAN'S BEST ARTISTS

Katsushika Hokusai

Master of Ukiyo-e (浮世絵), or the art of Japanese engravings between the 17th and 20th centuries, Hokusai was one of Japan's most prestigious artists and certainly the most international Japanese artist. His drawings, which arrived in Paris in the mid-19th century, fascinated and influenced all the Impressionists, from Monet to Van Gogh. But his influence does not end there: some say that without Hokusai, various artistic disciplines such as modern woodcut, graphic design, comics, manga and even tattooing would not be the same. An artist of legendary humility, he always considered himself “a simple apprentice” and signed his works with different names, such as “Shunro”, “Sori”, “Kako”, “Taito”, “Gakyonjin”, “Iitsu” and “Manji”. A hard worker, he would get up early and paint until late at night, drawing until the last day of his life. Despite being an old man, in his last days he became more and more energetic and spontaneous. He enjoyed great prestige in the Japanese artistic community and his prints reached the West, where young artists were able to capture his obvious and original genius.

Yayoi Kusama

Yayoi Kusama (born March 22, 1929, Matsumoto, Japan) is a Japanese artist who is a selfdescribed “obsessional artist,” known for her extensive use of polka dots and for her infinity installations. She employed painting, sculpture, performance art, and installations in a variety of styles, including Pop art and Minimalism. By her own account, Kusama began painting as a child, at about the time she began experiencing hallucinations that often involved fields of dots. Those hallucinations and the theme of dots would continue to inform her art throughout her career. She had little formal training, studying art only briefly (1948–49) at the Kyōto City Specialist School of Arts. Family conflict and the desire to become an artist drove her to move in 1957 to the United States, where she settled in New York City. Before leaving Japan, she destroyed many of her early paintings. Her early work in New York City included what she called “infinity net” paintings. Those consisted of thousands of tiny marks obsessively repeated across large canvases without regard for the edges of the canvas, as if they continued into infinity.

Takashi Murakami

Drawing from traditional Japanese painting, sci-fi, anime, and the global art market, Takashi Murakami creates paintings, sculptures, and films populated by repeated motifs and mutating characters of his own creation. His wide-ranging work embodies an intersection of pop culture, history, and fine art. Murakami earned a BA, MFA, and PhD from Tokyo University of the Arts, where he studied nihonga (traditional Japanese painting). In 1996 he established the Hiropon Factory, a studio/workshop that in subsequent years grew into an art production and artist management company, now known as Kaikai Kiki Co. Ltd. Since the early 1990s Murakami has invented characters that combine aspects of popular cartoons from Japan, Europe, and the US—from his first Mr. DOB, who sometimes serves as a stand-in for the artist himself, to various anime characters and smiling flowers, bears, and lions. These figures act as icons and symbols—hosts for more complex themes of violence, technology, and fantasy.

Hiroshi Sugimoto

Hiroshi Sugimoto is a renowned Japanese photographer born in Tokyo in 1948. He began his studies in politics and sociology at Saint Paul University (Tokyo), but in 1970 he moved to Los Angeles to study photography at the Art Center College of Design. He currently lives between New York and Tokyo. Influenced by the technique and aesthetics of Dadaism and Surrealism, Sugimoto has created several series of works that deal with the animate and inanimate, the real and the unreal, the past and the present, the concrete and the abstract, the visible and the invisible. His works are characterized by their visual beauty, use of natural light, shadows and purity of form and mastery of technique, rejecting digital technology in favor of traditional methods. To elaborate his exquisite black and white images he works with a large format camera.

Utagawa Kuniyoshi

In the last years of the Edo period in Japan, the artist Utagawa Kuniyoshi, like all the masters of Ukiyo-e, revolutionized not only Japanese art, but all world art when his very fresh proposals reached the eyes of French artists in the mid-nineteenth century on the occasion of the universal exhibitions. The Japanese engravings of masters such as Kuniyoshi can be considered the seed of modern art. Kuniyoshi was the son of a silk dyer. He helped his father in the business and this must have influenced his textile designs and the use of color in his prints. At the age of twelve he was already an attraction because of his good drawing skills and became an apprentice in the workshop of Utagawa Toyokuni. When he became independent, he began to create parodies and drawings of warriors and actors, as was the fashion. Also bijinga women (the pin-ups of the time). But he also discovered the importance of the effects of light and shadow of Western art, which he imitated in the oriental way. Of course, his style was based on textile patterns.
